Technologies for Extraction and Processing of Musical Information in Music Therapy: Microanalysis of Musical Traces and Possible Interfaces with Social Cognition

Abstract
Ivan Moriá Borges Rodrigues

Data collection for the analysis of music therapy sessions can be conducted in various ways. There is an abundance of qualitative studies that narrate the functioning of sessions observationally, supported by music therapy evaluation scales.

There is a lack of publications in Brazil that utilize quantitative methods for data collection and analysis in Music Therapy using digital equipment, such as musical instruments that transmit information via MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) to electronic processing.

This research project aims to identify the potential factors that hinder access to this form of data collection, explore ways to develop more accessible methods for use by music therapists in Brazil, and assess the feasibility of continuing to develop platforms that use MIDI technology for research. The methodology will be divided into two main workstreams.

In the first, participants will interact through a digital MIDI instrument that processes all the musical information played, with musical elements in audio. All information will be processed in a patch developed in MAX/MSP for data collection and in a Python script for quantitative data analysis.

In the second workstream, interviews will be conducted with music therapists who use technology in their practices, aiming to map the use of technological tools by music therapists and researchers and conduct an initial validity assessment of the material already developed.

This will include initial studies of face, content, and construct validity of the developed protocol, such as analyses of correlations between spontaneous time and anxiety, correlations between synchronization error rates and anticipation skills (related to social cognition), among other psychometric analyses.

We hope that this experiment will enable us to quantitatively visualize levels of perception among participants and the relationship between various musical stimuli and musical responses, specifically related to rhythmic aspects such as anticipation, delay, synchronization, perception, memory, and understanding of symbolic structures, among others.

This project will contribute to the development of new tools for data collection in Music Therapy, also identifying potential factors that hinder access to this form of data collection and exploring ways to develop more accessible methods for use by music therapists in Brazil.

Keywords: Music Information Retrieval, Music Therapy, Music and Technology, Music Therapy Analysis.

The MIR - MT Project


Given the vast potential for the collection and analysis of musical data from Music Therapy (MT) sessions using MIDI, through Music Information Retrieval (MIR) techniques, there are challenges in advancing technological methodologies and assessing subjective aspects of the therapeutic process.

This research project aims to identify factors that hinder access to this wealth of data, explore ways to make these tools more accessible for music therapists in Brazil, and investigate opportunities for the ongoing development of platforms that utilize MIDI technology for research.



Research Timeline

(2023 – 2024)  Research articles on the concept of MIR in Music Therapy, the use of MIDI data for research, and the use of technological tools in Music Therapy;

(2021 – 2025)  Development of programming languages such as MAX/MSP (Cycling ’74) and Python for processing MIDI data and creating visual and statistical analysis;

(2024 – 2025)  Advancement of an intuitive visual interface to ensure accessibility for all users and researchers;

(2024 – 2025)  Interviews with experts in music therapy to explore their use of technological tools and collect evaluative feedback on this ongoing research.

MAX/MSP

Patch developed by Ivan Moriá that selects the MIDI input and generates a list with the following information:

Velocity (to measure the strength of pressed notes), Key pressed and Duration of the key pressed (to estimate the density). There is also a velocity sequencer that splits the information into four levels of strength to provide visual feedback with colors and allow other interactions with this information, such as promoting interactive changing behaviors. All of this information is sent to a Coll object patch (the next one available for download) and then saved as a .csv list, to be processed in other environments such as Python scripts.

Patch with the main function of recording multiple audio buffers and easily playing them back by selecting the buffer number.

Recordings are triggered by a pedal, which automatically trims the buffer to the total duration of the file. It is also possible to simultaneously play multiple buffers and loop them, enabling live sound experimentation. The patch was designed for the concert for bandoneon and electronics by the Contemporary Music Research Center held in December 2023, as part of the Electroacoustic Music Workshop course taught by José Padovani and Rogério Vasconcelos.

The code implements a stopwatch application using PyQt5, with the TimerApp class inheriting from QWidget to create an interactive graphical

interface. TimerApp is useful for task timing, musical practice, and activity monitoring. Its modular structure allows for easy extensions, such as animations and additional functionalities. Timers and buttons make the interface intuitive and responsive, ensuring a pleasant user experience.


The code reads a DASS responses CSV file into a pandas DataFrame, which must contain two columns: one for questions and another for response numbers. It defines a function to

calculate scores for depression, anxiety, and stress, doubling the scores based on specific indices and classifying them into categorie (Normal, Mild, Moderate, Severe, Extremely Severe) using separate classification functions. The final results include the total scores and classifications for each psychological condition.

This code calculates various statistics on key press data, including average and standard deviation of velocities, frequency of each key press, BPM (beats per minute), time intervals, density of presses, and key variation. Functions

are modular and return a detailed dictionary with all metrics, providing insights into dynamics, timing, and rhythm patterns in key press data.
